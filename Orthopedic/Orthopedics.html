Orthopedics

Osteoarthritis

- Symptoms develop slowly and usually begin with one joint and include:

  • Swelling in and around the joint, especially after moving the joint a lot.
  • Impairment in the ability to move the joint.
  • Feeling that the joint is unstable.
  • Hearing a sound when moving the joint.
  • As symptoms get worse over time, some activities can be difficult to do.
  • Pain when using the joint, which may improve with rest. For some people, in the later stages of the disease, the pain may be worse at night.
  • Joint stiffness and usually lasts less than 30 minutes in the morning or after resting for a while.
  • The pain and other symptoms of osteoarthritis can make you feel tired, have trouble sleeping, and feel depressed.
- Treatment: Treatment goals for osteoarthritis include:
  • Reduce pain and other symptoms.
  • Improve joint function.
  • Prevent the disease from getting worse.
  • Maintain the quality of your life.

Rheumatoid Arthritis

- Symptoms: The patient with rheumatoid arthritis goes through
times when symptoms increase, and times when
they decrease, and its symptoms include:

  • Joint pain, including swelling for a long period of up to six weeks.
  • Joint stiffness for 30 minutes or more.
  • Fatigue, fever, and weight loss.
  • Increased infections.
  • Dry eyes and mouth.
  • Visual impairment.
  • Small lumps under the skin, forming on bony areas.
  • Inflammation of the lungs.
  • Vasculitis.
- Treatment: The purpose of treating rheumatoid arthritis:
  • Stop inflammation, relieve symptoms, and prevent joint and organ damage.
  • Improving physical function and reducing long-term complications.
  • Treatment is with non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs, drugs that slow down the disease, or inhibitors, and the last option is surgery.

Gout

- Symptoms:

  • Gout flares, also called gout attacks, are sudden attacks of severe joint pain. It usually causes redness, swelling, and a feeling of flushing in the joint. Although a gout attack usually affects one joint, some people develop infections in several joints at the same time. Episodes often begin at night, and in the early morning hours rather than during the day, but they can occur at any time.
  • The pain and inflammation usually peak within 12 to 24 hours, and the patient's condition improves within a few days to several weeks, even if the disease is not treated.
- Treatment: Treatment for gout attacks aims to reduce pain and inflammation, and reduce the incidence of complications quickly and safely. This treatment is usually short-term, and limited to the duration of the flare. Deciding which medications to use depends on several factors, such as: bleeding risk, kidney health, and a history of stomach or small intestine ulcers.
  • Anti-inflammatory medications such as ibuprofen and indomethacin are the best treatment for gout attacks. These medications are most effective when started early in the attack. These medications reduce swelling in the joint. And when there is a history of gout, the doctor can prescribe medication in the event of a flare-up. Early treatment is important in shortening the duration of pain and reducing the intensity and duration of flares.
  • Preventive treatment: It aims to prevent or reduce the occurrence of gout attacks. The treatment is prescribed - usually - by the doctor, and it is taken daily in small doses; To avoid gastrointestinal side effects.

Rickets

- Symptoms:

  • Softness in the skull and delayed closing of the fontanelles
  • Pain and swelling in the joints.
  • Expansion of the end of the ribs (Rachitic rosary)
  • Deformation of the bones, legs and knees.
  • Delayed or swaying in walking.
  • Imbalance in growth, short stature and lack of weight gain.
  • Dental abnormalities.
  • Sometimes fractures occur.
